## Start With the Total Number

Before you look at venues or dream about details, establish the total amount you're willing and able to spend. This number should come from three sources:

1. **Your own savings** — what you have now plus what you'll save before the wedding
2. **Confirmed family contributions** — money that has been formally offered and committed
3. **Any financing** — personal loans or credit, though going into debt for a wedding is rarely advisable

Be conservative. If a family member has said they "might" contribute, don't count it until you have a specific number confirmed.

## Typical Budget Allocations

While every wedding is different, here's how costs tend to break down:

| Category | Typical % of Budget |
|---|---|
| Venue + catering | 40–50% |
| Photography + video | 10–12% |
| Music (DJ or band) | 5–10% |
| Flowers + décor | 8–10% |
| Wedding attire | 5–8% |
| Stationery + printing | 2–3% |
| Officiant | 1–2% |
| Transportation | 2–3% |
| Hair + makeup | 2–3% |
| Cake + desserts | 2–3% |
| Miscellaneous + buffer | 5–8% |

The venue and catering consistently dominate wedding budgets — and are often the category where couples most significantly overspend. Lock this number down first.

## The Buffer Rule

Always reserve 5–10% of your total budget as a buffer. Unexpected costs are the rule, not the exception:

- Tax and service charges not mentioned in initial quotes (can add 25–35% to food and beverage costs)
- Vendor overtime fees
- Day-of supplies and forgotten items
- Gratuities for vendors (a real and expected cost)

If you spend the buffer, you were on budget. If you don't spend it, you have a nice honeymoon top-up.

## The Per-Guest Math

One of the most useful planning tools is calculating your per-person cost. Most venue + catering packages are priced per head. If your venue costs $150/person and you're planning for 120 guests, that's $18,000 before alcohol, tax, and service charges.

To reduce total cost, the most effective lever is **reducing guest count**. Cutting 20 people from a 150-person wedding often saves more than any other single decision.

## Avoiding the Three Most Common Overspend Traps

### 1. The "Just a Little More" Upgrade
Every vendor will offer upgrades. The upgraded package is usually only $X more. These additions compound. Set a rule: any upgrade must displace something else in the budget, not just add to it.

### 2. Booking Before Seeing Full Costs
Many venues quote a per-person food price without prominently advertising the venue rental fee, mandatory bar minimums, service charges, and tax. Always ask for a complete all-in estimate before committing.

### 3. The DIY Cost Illusion
DIY can save money, but it often doesn't. Factor in your time, materials, storage, and transport before assuming it's cheaper. DIY florals, for example, require sourcing, cold storage, and significant assembly time — often on the day before your wedding when you'd rather be resting.

## Getting Quotes: What to Ask

When requesting quotes from vendors, always ask:

- What is the total cost including all fees, tax, and service charges?
- What is the payment schedule and cancellation policy?
- What is included vs. what costs extra?
- Do you have packages at different price points?
- Is there a price-lock guarantee if we book early?

Getting these answers in writing protects you from surprises.

## Tracking Your Budget

Use a spreadsheet with three columns for every line item: **budgeted**, **quoted**, and **actual**. Update it every time you get a quote or make a payment. Review it monthly.

Free tools like Google Sheets work as well as any paid wedding budgeting app — the discipline of updating it regularly matters more than the tool.

## When the Budget Feels Impossible

If the wedding you want costs significantly more than you have, the honest answer is one of three things:

1. **Reduce the guest count** — the single most impactful lever
2. **Change the format** — a brunch wedding, a Friday wedding, or an off-season wedding can cost 20–40% less than a Saturday evening in peak season
3. **Wait and save longer** — getting married a year later with a realistic budget beats starting married life in debt

Your wedding should be the beginning of your life together, not a financial burden on it.
