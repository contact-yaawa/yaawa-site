## The Core Question: Coworker or Actual Friend?

The most useful filter is simple: would you make plans with this person outside of work if you didn't work together? If the answer is yes, they're a friend who happens to be a coworker. If the answer is no, they're a work acquaintance — and you have no obligation to invite them.

This distinction matters because wedding guest lists should reflect your actual relationships, not your org chart.

## The All-or-Nothing Approach

Some couples choose to either invite all coworkers from their immediate team or none at all. This approach has real appeal:

- **No one feels singled out** — everyone gets the same treatment
- **Simpler logic** — one rule for the whole workplace
- **Avoids political fallout** — no speculation about who made the cut

The downside is that you may end up inviting colleagues you barely know while excluding closer friends to stay within budget.

## Managing Workplace Discretion

If you're inviting some coworkers and not others, keep the wedding relatively private at work. Practical steps:

- Don't discuss wedding details openly in shared spaces
- Ask invited coworkers to be discreet about the invitation
- If asked directly, you can honestly say "We kept it small — just close friends and family"

You're not obligated to explain your guest list to anyone. Most mature adults understand that weddings have budgets.

## When the Boss Factor Matters

Deciding whether to invite your manager or boss adds another layer. Consider:

- **If you're genuinely close**, invite them as you would any other friend
- **If it feels obligatory**, don't — awkward power dynamics at a personal event rarely end well
- **If you invite your boss**, consider whether skipping subordinates or peers will cause issues

There's no requirement to invite up the chain of command.

## The Bottom Line

Your wedding is a personal celebration. Guest lists reflect genuine relationships, not professional courtesy. Invite the people who matter to you, be thoughtful about workplace discretion, and don't feel guilty about keeping it personal.
