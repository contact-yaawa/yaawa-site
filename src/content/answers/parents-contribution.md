## The Old Rules vs. Reality

Traditionally, wedding cost responsibility was divided: the bride's family paid for most of the wedding, the groom's family paid for the rehearsal dinner, and the couple covered honeymoon expenses. In practice today, these rules rarely apply.

Modern wedding funding typically comes from some mix of:
- The couple themselves (increasingly common)
- Both sets of parents contributing to the main event
- One set of parents covering specific elements
- No family contribution at all

None of these arrangements is inherently right or wrong.

## What Parents Actually Contribute

There's no reliable average because contributions vary so dramatically. Some parents offer nothing; others offer to cover the entire wedding. Factors that influence contributions:

- **Family finances** — the most obvious factor
- **Cultural expectations** — in many cultures, family-funded weddings are the norm
- **Number of children** — parents who funded five previous weddings may have less to offer
- **Relationship quality** — estrangement or tension may reduce or eliminate contributions
- **Their own retirement security** — responsible parents prioritize their own financial stability

If parents offer nothing, it may have nothing to do with how they feel about you or the marriage.

## How to Have the Conversation

The money conversation is uncomfortable, but it's far better to have it early than to discover the truth after you've booked a venue you can't afford alone.

**When to ask:** Within the first few weeks of engagement, before you've made any financial commitments.

**How to frame it:** Don't ask "how much are you giving us?" Instead, try:

> "We're starting to think about the wedding budget. We don't want to assume anything, but we wanted to ask if you were planning to contribute, and if so, roughly what you had in mind. Whatever the answer, we'll plan accordingly."

This framing removes pressure while opening the door.

## When Contribution Comes With Conditions

Money from family often comes with opinions. A parent contributing $20,000 may feel entitled to input on the guest list, venue choice, or ceremony details. This is a real dynamic and worth discussing before accepting.

You have three options:

1. **Accept the money and the influence** — sometimes family input is genuinely welcome
2. **Accept the money, negotiate the terms** — "We appreciate your contribution and value your input, but we'll make final decisions"
3. **Decline or reduce the contribution** — financial independence is worth the trade-off for some couples

Clarity upfront prevents resentment on both sides.

## Unequal Contributions Between Families

It's common for one family to contribute significantly more than the other. This can create subtle (or not so subtle) tension. To manage it:

- Keep contribution amounts private between families when possible
- Don't allow disproportionate contributions to translate into disproportionate guest list control
- Acknowledge and express genuine gratitude to both families equally

## The Bottom Line

Parental contributions are a bonus, not a given. Plan a wedding you can afford on your own resources. If family money materializes, it expands your options. If it doesn't, you're not in debt. Have the conversation early, get clarity on terms, and only count money that's been formally confirmed.
