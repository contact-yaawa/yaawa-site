## The Traditional Etiquette View

Traditional wedding etiquette holds that when you invite someone to a celebration, you are responsible for hosting them — and that includes providing food and drinks. In this framework, asking guests to pay for their own alcohol is considered a breach of hospitality, similar to asking them to contribute to their own dinner.

This view is most strongly held in the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom, where open bars at receptions have become a strong cultural expectation. In some European countries, cash bars at weddings are entirely normal and carry no stigma.

## Why Couples Choose Cash Bars

Alcohol service is one of the most significant line items in a wedding budget. A full open bar for 100 guests can easily cost $3,000–$8,000 depending on the venue, location, and duration. For couples on a tight budget, eliminating or reducing this cost isn't a sign of disrespect — it's financial reality.

Other reasons couples opt for cash bars:

- **Religious or personal values** — some couples simply don't want alcohol at their wedding
- **Venue restrictions** — some venues only permit cash bar setups
- **Family dynamics** — a history of alcohol-related family issues may make limiting drinking the right call

## The Modern Perspective

Attitudes toward cash bars have shifted meaningfully in the past decade. More guests understand that weddings are expensive, and more couples are transparent about their choices. What matters most is **communication and hospitality in other areas**.

A couple who is warm, generous with food, attentive to guests, and upfront about the cash bar on their wedding website will receive far less judgment than one who surprises guests at the event.

## Alternatives That Satisfy Both Sides

If a full open bar isn't feasible, these middle-ground options are widely accepted:

- **Beer and wine only** — covers most guests' preferences at a fraction of the cost
- **Signature cocktails + beer/wine** — adds a personal touch without full bar pricing
- **Open bar during cocktail hour only** — then switch to cash bar or cut alcohol
- **Drink tickets** — give each guest 2–3 tickets for free drinks, cash after that
- **Dry wedding with exceptional food** — if no alcohol at all, invest heavily in the food and entertainment

## The Bottom Line

There is no universal answer. What matters most is being honest with your guests and making them feel genuinely welcomed in every other way. If you choose a cash bar, say so on your wedding website — never leave guests to discover it at the event.
